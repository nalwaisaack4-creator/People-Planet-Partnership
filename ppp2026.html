About People Planet Partnership

Our journey, vision, and the team behind our mission

Our Story

Founded in 2021, People Planet Partnership was born from a simple realisation: environmental sustainability and human wellbeing are inseparable. We cannot protect the planet without caring for its people, and we cannot support communities without preserving their natural environments.

Our journey began when co‑founders Yael Cherotich and Isaack Nalwa witnessed firsthand how environmental degradation disproportionately affects women and marginalised communities in East Africa. They saw innovative local solutions combining traditional knowledge with modern techniques, but these initiatives often lacked support, resources, and recognition.

Our Approach

Community‑Centered Design: We spend weeks listening before co‑designing solutions.
Women‑Led Innovation: We prioritise women's leadership – they bear the greatest burden while holding unique knowledge.
Youth Empowerment: Young people are today's innovators; we create spaces for them to lead climate action.

Women-Led Water Security Initiative

In regions facing increasing water scarcity, women bear the primary responsibility for water collection. Our program trains women in sustainable water management, empowering them as community water stewards.

Started: March 2021 Kenya, Tanzania 500+ women trained Ongoing

The Challenge

In East Africa, climate change makes rainfall unpredictable. Traditional water sources dry up; women and girls walk up to 6 hours daily for water. This affects health, safety, economic opportunities, and girls' education. Yet women – most affected – are rarely included in water management decisions.

Our Approach

Phase 1: Community Assessment

  • Map water sources & vulnerabilities
  • Identify traditional knowledge
  • Understand gender dynamics
  • Assess climate risks

Phase 2: Women's Water Leadership Training

  • Rainwater harvesting
  • Conservation & purification
  • Infrastructure maintenance
  • Climate adaptation
  • Leadership & organising

Phase 3: Community Implementation

  • Rainwater systems construction
  • Spring protection
  • Water committees
  • Management plans

Impact Stories

Mama Aisha's Transformation

Makueni County, Kenya. Mama Aisha, mother of five, used to spend 4‑6 hours daily collecting water. After training: installed rainwater harvesting, trained 15 other women, established a water committee, now runs a nursery selling drought‑resistant seedlings. “Before, water was my burden. Now, it's my business.”

The Spring Protection Project

Arusha, Tanzania. Trained women identified a contaminated seasonal spring. They organised the community to protect the catchment, built a simple filtration system, established maintenance schedules. Waterborne diseases reduced by 70%.

Program Highlights

  • 500+ women trained
  • 85 rainwater systems installed
  • 12 water committees established
  • 60% reduction in water collection time
  • 40% increase in girls' school attendance

Looking Ahead

  • Integration with agricultural water management
  • Solar‑powered pumping
  • Water credit systems
  • National policy advocacy for gender‑responsive water governance

Green Entrepreneurship Incubator Program

A 6‑month intensive program providing women entrepreneurs with training, mentorship, and seed funding to launch environmentally sustainable businesses that address local challenges.

Started: August 2022 Uganda, Rwanda 45 women entrepreneurs Ongoing

The Opportunity

Women entrepreneurs in East Africa face barriers: limited capital, training, networks, and markets. Yet they are natural innovators, creating businesses that solve community problems while protecting the environment. Our incubator links environmental sustainability and economic empowerment.

Program Structure

Month 1‑2: Foundation Building

  • Market research & validation
  • Sustainable business model
  • Environmental impact assessment
  • Financial literacy

Month 3‑4: Skill Development

  • Product development & quality
  • Marketing & branding
  • Digital skills & e‑commerce
  • Supply chain management
  • Pitching & investor readiness

Month 5‑6: Launch & Growth

  • One‑on‑one mentorship
  • Seed funding (up to $2,000)
  • Networks & market linkages
  • Peer support

Success Stories

Eco‑Straws by Grace

Kampala, Uganda. Grace, a former teacher, noticed plastic straw pollution. She launched edible straws made from sorghum and millet. After 6 months: sold 50,000+ straws, created 8 jobs, reduced plastic waste equivalent to 200,000 straws, featured in national media.

Solar Sisters Collective

Kigali, Rwanda. Three women created a pay‑as‑you‑go solar leasing model. Achievements: 150 households with solar power, 10 women trained as technicians, 90% reduction in kerosene use, expanding to neighboring districts.

Program Impact

  • 45 women graduated
  • 15 green businesses launched
  • 85 new jobs (65% women)
  • $150,000+ combined revenue
  • 100% businesses incorporate environmental sustainability

Erasmus+ Youth Exchange Programs

Bringing together young environmental leaders from Europe and Africa for cross‑cultural learning, skill development, and collaborative projects.

Started: September 2022 Kenya, Germany, Belgium 120+ youth Annual program

Why International Youth Exchanges Matter

Climate change knows no borders. Our exchanges create magic when a young woman from Nairobi shares water conservation techniques with a student from Berlin, or when a German engineering student helps design solar‑powered irrigation for a Kenyan farm.

Our Approach

Phase 1: Pre‑Exchange (3 months virtual)

  • Learn cultures & contexts
  • Identify shared challenges
  • Collaborative research
  • Build trust

Phase 2: Immersion (3 weeks)

  • Live with host families
  • Visit environmental projects
  • Hands‑on workshops (permaculture, renewables, community organising)
  • Co‑create action plans

Phase 3: Post‑Exchange Support

  • 6 months mentorship
  • Small grants for community projects
  • Virtual check‑ins
  • Presentations to stakeholders

Impact Stories

Solar Sisterhood

Sarah (Kenya) and Lena (Germany) addressed energy poverty in women‑led households. They developed “Solar Sisterhood”: trains women as solar technicians, creates micro‑enterprises, brought solar power to 150 households, now in 3 countries.

Food Forest Connection

David (Belgium) and Wanjiru (Kenya) combined Belgian agroforestry with Kenyan indigenous knowledge. Result: food forest design, training for 40 farmers, 300% biodiversity increase, improved food security for 200 families.

Program Highlights

  • 120+ youth from 8 countries
  • 15 collaborative projects sustained
  • 85% report increased leadership confidence
  • 70% projects continue beyond program year
  • 40% secured additional funding

Why Women's Leadership is Critical for Climate Resilience

Exploring the unique contributions women leaders make to climate adaptation

Back to Blog
May 15, 2023 Yael Cherotich 5 min read Women's Leadership, Climate Adaptation

When we talk about climate change, we often discuss melting glaciers, rising sea levels, and extreme weather events. But there's another story that needs telling—one about the women who are quietly, persistently building resilience in their communities.

In my eight years working across East Africa, I've noticed something powerful: communities where women lead adaptation efforts fare better. They recover faster from climate shocks. They innovate more creatively. They build stronger networks.

The Data Tells a Story

A 2021 UN study found that when women are included in climate decision-making, communities adopt more effective and sustainable adaptation strategies. Yet women remain dramatically underrepresented in climate leadership roles.

Women's Unique Perspective

  • Water collection: women feel water scarcity first and most acutely
  • Food security: they understand what crops fail and why
  • Care work: they see health impacts of heatwaves and disease
  • Small‑scale agriculture: they experiment with climate‑smart techniques
“When women lead, they don't just solve problems—they transform systems. They ask different questions, prioritise different needs, and build different kinds of solutions.”

Real-World Examples

Mama Aisha's Water Collective

In northern Kenya, Mama Aisha organised women to map water sources, create a rotating schedule, train in rainwater harvesting, and start a repair fund. Within six months, daily water collection time dropped by 60%, girls' school attendance increased, and women had more time for income generation.

The Green Grandmothers Network

In western Uganda, grandmothers preserved 23 traditional crop varieties through seed‑saving collectives, now vital as rainfall becomes unpredictable.

The “Why” Behind the Success

  1. Collective well‑being: women prioritise community impacts and long‑term sustainability.
  2. Trust networks: horizontal networks of support prove resilient during crises.
  3. Innovate within constraints: low‑cost, accessible solutions.
  4. Bridge formal & informal systems: integrated solutions.

The Challenge: Breaking Barriers

  • Funding: only 0.01% of global funding supports climate + women's rights projects.
  • Representation: women hold only 15% of environment minister positions.
  • Resource access: women farmers receive 10% of agricultural aid.
  • Cultural norms: participation still discouraged.

What We Can Do

Organisations: create funding streams for women‑led climate initiatives, mentorship programs.
Communities: value women's environmental knowledge, support participation.
Individuals: amplify voices, support women‑led organisations, challenge stereotypes.

Conclusion: A More Resilient Future

Climate resilience has a woman's face. It's time we recognised it, supported it, and followed where it leads.

Lessons from Our First Green Entrepreneurship Cohort

Reflections on launching women-led eco‑businesses in East Africa

Back to Blog
April 28, 2023 Isaack Nalwa 7 min read Entrepreneurship, Green Business, Women Empowerment

Six months ago, we launched our first Green Entrepreneurship Incubator with 15 women entrepreneurs from Uganda and Rwanda. Here are the key lessons we learned.

Diverse Ideas, Common Challenges

  • Access to capital: most rejected by banks
  • Market access: limited networks
  • Technical skills: business planning, financial management
  • Balancing responsibilities: family and community obligations

Lesson 1: Trust-Based Funding Works

We provided seed funding (up to $2,000) in tranches based on mutually agreed milestones – no collateral. 100% used funds as intended. “For the first time, someone believed in my idea enough to give me capital without asking for my house as collateral.” – Grace, Eco‑Straws Founder.

Lesson 2: Peer Learning is Powerful

Weekly cohort meetings led to three collaborative ventures, including the “Solar Sisters Collective”.

Lesson 3: Environmental Impact Drives Economic Success

Sarah's reusable sanitary pads from organic cotton – she emphasised environmental benefits (waste reduction) and health (chemical‑free), attracting customers willing to pay premium.

Lesson 4: Digital Skills Open New Markets

Training in WhatsApp Business, Facebook Marketplace, simple websites, basic photography. Average sales increased by 40%.

Lesson 5: Family Support is Crucial

Family orientation sessions turned husbands into childcare supporters and sometimes business partners.

The Results

  • 15 businesses launched – all environmentally sustainable
  • 85 new jobs (65% women)
  • $150,000+ combined revenue
  • 100% plan to continue
  • 3 collaborative ventures

Looking Ahead

Adding “family ambassador” roles, alumni network, partnerships with local banks, green business certification.

Bridging Generational Knowledge for Sustainable Agriculture

How traditional wisdom and modern science can work together for food security

Back to Blog
March 10, 2023 Guest Writer 6 min read Agriculture, Traditional Knowledge, Food Security

75‑year‑old Mama Atieno predicts rains by observing ants and flowering trees. 25‑year‑old Samuel uses weather apps and soil sensors. For years, these knowledge systems existed in parallel. Our intergenerational dialogue forums are changing that.

The Wisdom of Elders

  • Seed selection & preservation – local adaptation
  • Natural pest control – local plants as repellents
  • Water conservation – traditional irrigation
  • Seasonal calendars – reading natural signs

The Energy of Youth

  • Digital literacy – information & markets
  • Formal education – scientific principles
  • Innovation mindset – experimentation
  • Global perspective – trends from other regions

Our Approach: Dialogue, Not Teaching

  • Farm walks: elders lead, youth document with smartphones
  • Innovation labs: youth demonstrate modern tech, elders adapt
  • Story circles: elders share past adaptations, youth share climate projections
“I used to think my grandfather's farming methods were backward. Now I see they're based on deep observation of nature. Combined with modern science, they could be revolutionary.” – Samuel

Success Stories

The Three Sisters Garden Revival

Corn‑beans‑squash planting – elders remembered, youth researched scientific benefits (nitrogen fixation, pest reduction, soil conservation). Yields increased 30% while reducing fertiliser use.

Digital Seed Exchange

Elders identified rare, drought‑resistant varieties; youth created digital catalog and trading platform. Now connects farmers across East Africa, preserves biodiversity, creates economic opportunities.

Weather Prediction Hybrid Model

Combining traditional signs with modern forecasting apps – 85% accuracy for seasonal rains, compared to 60% for modern forecasts alone.

Impact

  • 42% increase in sustainable farming practices
  • 67% reduction in chemical fertiliser/pesticide use
  • 28% increase in crop diversity
  • Improved social cohesion between generations

The Way Forward

Youth‑led documentation of traditional knowledge, elder advisory councils, cross‑community exchanges, policy advocacy for recognition of traditional knowledge.
